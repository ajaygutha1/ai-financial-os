# Diversification and Asset Allocation Basics

## What diversification actually protects against

Diversification means spreading money across many different investments
(and different *kinds* of investments) so that no single company, sector,
or event can severely damage the whole portfolio. It doesn't increase
expected returns on its own — its job is to reduce the range of outcomes,
particularly the chance of a catastrophic loss, for a given level of
expected return.

A common failure mode is **concentration risk**: holding a large fraction
of net worth in a single company's stock, often an employer's stock through
equity compensation or a 401(k) default. This is riskier than it feels,
because a job and a large chunk of net worth become correlated — if the
company has serious trouble, both the paycheck and the portfolio can be hit
at the same time.

## Asset classes

The broadest diversification decision is the mix across asset classes,
each with different risk/return characteristics and different behavior in
different economic conditions:

- **Stocks (equities)**: ownership stakes in companies. Higher expected
  long-run return, higher volatility.
- **Bonds (fixed income)**: loans to governments or companies. Generally
  lower expected return than stocks, lower volatility, and often (not
  always) move differently than stocks during downturns.
- **Cash and cash equivalents**: savings accounts, money market funds,
  short-term Treasuries. Lowest expected return, lowest volatility, highest
  liquidity.
- **Real estate**: direct property ownership or REITs (real estate
  investment trusts). Return and risk profile depends heavily on the
  specific investment; adds exposure to a different set of economic
  factors than stocks or bonds.
- **Alternatives** (commodities, private equity, etc.): typically a smaller
  allocation for most individual investors, used for further
  diversification rather than as a core holding.

## Diversifying within an asset class

Within stocks specifically, diversification also means spreading across:

- **Company size** (large-, mid-, and small-cap)
- **Geography** (domestic and international)
- **Sector** (technology, healthcare, financials, energy, etc.)

A single low-cost broad-market index fund often already provides
substantial diversification across company and sector, since it holds
hundreds or thousands of companies in proportion to the overall market —
this is why "just buy a total market index fund" is common, simple advice
for investors who don't want to actively manage sector or stock-picking
decisions.

## Allocation shifts with time horizon and risk tolerance

The right mix between stocks, bonds, and cash depends on when the money is
needed and how much volatility someone can tolerate without making poor
decisions under stress:

- **Long time horizon** (retirement decades away): can typically afford a
  higher stock allocation, since there's time to recover from a downturn.
- **Short time horizon** (money needed within a few years): should
  typically favor bonds and cash, since there's little time to recover from
  a bad sequence of returns right before the money is needed.

A common heuristic (one starting point among several, not a rule) is to
hold a bond percentage roughly equal to age, with the rest in stocks —
becoming more conservative gradually over time. Actual target allocations
vary widely based on individual goals, other assets, and risk tolerance,
and this heuristic is a rough starting point for discussion, not a
personalized recommendation.

## Rebalancing

Because different asset classes grow at different rates, a portfolio's
actual allocation drifts away from its target over time (e.g. a strong
stock market can leave someone with a much higher stock percentage than
originally intended). **Rebalancing** — periodically selling some of what
has grown to buy back into what has lagged, to restore the target mix — is
how a portfolio stays aligned with its intended risk level over time.
