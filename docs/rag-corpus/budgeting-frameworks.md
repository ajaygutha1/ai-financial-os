# Budgeting Frameworks

## 50/30/20

A simple starting framework for allocating after-tax income:

- **50% Needs**: rent/mortgage, utilities, groceries, insurance, minimum
  debt payments, transportation to work — costs that don't disappear even
  if spending is cut aggressively.
- **30% Wants**: dining out, entertainment, subscriptions, travel,
  non-essential shopping — discretionary spending that meaningfully
  improves quality of life but could be reduced if needed.
- **20% Savings and debt payoff**: retirement contributions, emergency fund
  building, extra debt payments beyond the minimum, and other investing.

This is a rough starting point, not a strict rule — high cost-of-living
areas often push "needs" well above 50%, and someone aggressively pursuing
an early financial goal might intentionally push the savings percentage
much higher than 20%. Its main value is as a quick sanity check: if
"wants" is consuming 50%+ of income while savings is near 0%, that's a
signal worth addressing regardless of the exact target percentages.

## Zero-based budgeting

Every dollar of income is assigned a specific job — a spending category, a
savings goal, or debt payoff — until income minus allocations equals zero.
Unlike 50/30/20's broad buckets, zero-based budgeting tracks spending at a
much more granular level (e.g. "groceries," "gas," "streaming
subscriptions" as separate line items). It requires more ongoing effort to
maintain but gives the most visibility into exactly where money goes, and
is often recommended for people who feel like they don't know where their
money went at the end of the month.

## Pay-yourself-first

Rather than budgeting spending first and saving whatever is left over,
pay-yourself-first automates savings and investing contributions
immediately when income arrives (e.g. an automatic transfer on payday, or
payroll-deducted retirement contributions), and the remainder is what's
available to spend. This removes the risk of "there's nothing left to
save" at the end of the month and works well combined with either of the
frameworks above for whatever spending is left after the automated
transfer.

## Recurring charges deserve periodic review

Subscriptions and other recurring charges are easy to lose track of
because each individual charge is often small — but the combined monthly
total can be substantial, and unlike a one-time purchase, the cost repeats
indefinitely until actively canceled. A periodic review (e.g. quarterly) of
every recurring charge, asking "would I sign up for this today at this
price," catches subscriptions that quietly outlived their usefulness far
more effectively than trying to remember to cancel things in the moment.

## Matching the framework to the person

No single budgeting framework is universally best. A framework that's too
detailed for someone's habits gets abandoned; a framework that's too loose
for someone who needs structure doesn't change behavior. The most effective
framework is the simplest one a person will actually maintain
consistently, not the theoretically most precise one.
