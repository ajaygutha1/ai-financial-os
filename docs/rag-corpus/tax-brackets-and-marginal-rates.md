# How Federal Income Tax Brackets Actually Work

**Important:** exact bracket thresholds and the standard deduction are set
annually by the IRS and adjusted for inflation most years. The dollar
figures below are illustrative examples from a recent tax year, not a
verified current-year source. Always confirm the actual current-year
brackets and standard deduction directly from IRS.gov (or a current IRS
Form 1040 instruction booklet) before using specific numbers for a real
filing or projection — do not treat the numbers in this document as
authoritative for the current tax year.

## Marginal, not flat

The U.S. federal income tax system is **progressive** and uses **marginal**
tax brackets. This is the single most commonly misunderstood part of the
system: moving into a higher bracket does **not** mean all of your income is
now taxed at that higher rate. Only the income that falls *within* each
bracket's range is taxed at that bracket's rate.

For example, using a simplified single-filer schedule with brackets at 10%,
12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, and 37% (illustrative, not current-year figures):
someone with $60,000 of taxable income doesn't pay 22% on the full $60,000
just because their income reaches the 22% bracket. They pay 10% on the
first slice, 12% on the next slice, and 22% only on the portion that falls
within the 22% bracket's range. Their **effective tax rate** (total tax
divided by total income) ends up meaningfully lower than their **marginal
rate** (the rate on their last dollar earned).

## Why this matters for decisions

Because only the marginal slice is taxed at the higher rate, a raise, bonus,
or extra freelance income that pushes someone into a higher bracket almost
never makes them worse off overall — a common but incorrect fear. It also
means pre-tax retirement contributions (traditional 401(k)/IRA) are most
valuable when they reduce income that would otherwise be taxed at the
**top** marginal rate a person is currently paying, not their effective
rate.

## Taxable income vs. gross income

Taxable income is not the same as gross (total) income. The **standard
deduction** (or itemized deductions, if higher) is subtracted from gross
income first, along with certain other adjustments, to arrive at taxable
income — the amount the bracket schedule actually applies to. Pre-tax
retirement contributions and HSA contributions also reduce taxable income
directly.

## Filing status changes the brackets

The dollar thresholds for each bracket differ by filing status — single,
married filing jointly, married filing separately, and head of household
each have their own bracket schedule, not just a simple doubling for joint
filers. Always use the schedule for the correct filing status.

## Capital gains are taxed differently

Long-term capital gains (on investments held more than one year) and
qualified dividends are taxed under a **separate**, generally lower rate
schedule than ordinary income (common long-term capital gains rates are
0%, 15%, and 20%, depending on total income) — they are not simply added
to ordinary income and taxed at the ordinary marginal rate. Short-term
capital gains (assets held one year or less), by contrast, are taxed as
ordinary income at the regular marginal brackets.
