# Fraud Prevention Basics

## What a duplicate or suspicious charge usually means

A charge appearing twice at the same merchant, for the same amount, within a
few days is most often one of: a merchant accidentally submitting an
authorization twice, a pending authorization and its settlement both showing
as separate line items temporarily, or a genuine double charge that a
merchant will reverse on request. It is worth confirming with the merchant or
card issuer before assuming malicious activity, but it is always worth
confirming rather than ignoring.

## Unusually large charges for a category

A single charge much larger than a person's typical spending in a given
category (dining, groceries, subscriptions) is not proof of fraud by itself —
it could be a legitimate one-off purchase — but it is exactly the kind of
signal worth a quick manual review, since fraudulent charges often stand out
by being disproportionately large relative to a person's normal pattern.

## A large first-time charge from an unfamiliar merchant

The combination of "never charged here before" and "unusually large amount"
is a stronger signal than either alone. Legitimate new large purchases happen
constantly, but so does card-testing and unauthorized use, which frequently
starts with an unfamiliar merchant name and an atypical amount.

## What to do when something looks off

- Check the transaction against personal records (a receipt, an order
  confirmation email) before disputing.
- If it's unrecognized, contact the card issuer or bank promptly — most
  regulated card networks have zero-liability policies for confirmed
  unauthorized charges, but timely reporting matters.
- Freezing or locking a card (most issuers support this instantly via app or
  website) stops new authorizations while a specific charge is investigated,
  without necessarily requiring a full card replacement.
- Recurring monitoring (reviewing statements regularly rather than only when
  something feels wrong) catches most fraud faster than waiting for a
  monthly statement review.

## What this guidance is not

None of the above is a determination that any specific transaction is
fraudulent — flagged patterns are a starting point for a person to review,
not a conclusion. Automated pattern-matching over a short transaction window
also cannot see a person's full history or intent, so false positives
(legitimate large or first-time purchases) are expected and normal.
