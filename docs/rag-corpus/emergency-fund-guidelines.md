# Emergency Fund Guidelines

## What an emergency fund is for

An emergency fund is cash held specifically to cover unplanned expenses or a
loss of income — a job loss, an urgent medical bill, a car repair, an
unexpected home repair — without having to sell investments at a bad time or
take on high-interest debt. Its job is to be available immediately, not to
earn the highest possible return.

## How much to keep

The standard guidance is **3 to 6 months of essential living expenses** —
rent or mortgage, utilities, groceries, insurance, minimum debt payments,
and other non-discretionary costs. Where in that range to land depends on
income stability:

- **3 months** is often reasonable for a household with two stable incomes,
  low job-loss risk, and good insurance coverage.
- **6 months or more** is more appropriate for a single income earner,
  variable or commission-based income, self-employment, or a household in
  an industry with higher layoff risk.

Some more conservative guidance extends this to **9-12 months** for highly
variable income (e.g. commission-only sales, freelance work) or when
supporting dependents with significant fixed costs.

## Where to keep it

Emergency funds should be liquid and low-risk: a high-yield savings
account, a money market account, or short-term Treasury bills. The point is
guaranteed availability, not growth — money that might be needed on short
notice should not be invested in the stock market, where a downturn could
force selling at a loss exactly when the cash is needed.

## Building it up

If a full emergency fund isn't in place yet, a common approach is to
automate a fixed transfer each payday until the target is reached, treating
it like a non-negotiable bill rather than something saved only if money is
left over at the end of the month. Even a partial cushion (one month of
expenses) meaningfully reduces the chance of going into debt over a small
emergency, so building it incrementally is far better than waiting until a
lump sum is available.

## When it's "done"

Once the target range is reached, ongoing surplus cash flow is generally
better directed toward higher-interest debt payoff or long-term investing,
since idle cash beyond the emergency-fund target loses purchasing power to
inflation over time. Revisit the target periodically — a higher cost of
living, new dependents, or a shift to less stable income are all reasons to
recalculate the target rather than assuming the original number still
applies.
