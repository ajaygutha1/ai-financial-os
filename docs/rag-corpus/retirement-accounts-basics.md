# Retirement Accounts Basics (U.S.)

## The core account types

**401(k) / 403(b)**: employer-sponsored retirement accounts. Contributions
are typically payroll-deducted. Many employers offer a **match** — extra
money the employer contributes based on how much the employee contributes,
up to a limit (e.g. "50% match up to 6% of salary"). An employer match is
effectively free money and is generally the single highest-priority use of
retirement savings, since no other account or investment offers a
guaranteed, immediate return like a match does.

**Traditional IRA**: an individual retirement account funded with
pre-tax-eligible contributions (subject to income limits if also covered by
a workplace plan). Contributions may reduce taxable income now; withdrawals
in retirement are taxed as ordinary income.

**Roth IRA / Roth 401(k)**: funded with after-tax contributions (subject to
income limits for a Roth IRA). No tax deduction now, but qualified
withdrawals in retirement — including all investment growth — are
completely tax-free. Generally favored by people who expect to be in a
similar or higher tax bracket in retirement than they are now, or who value
tax-free growth and more flexible early-withdrawal rules on contributions.

**HSA (Health Savings Account)**: not strictly a retirement account, but
functions as an exceptionally efficient one for the eligible (those on a
qualifying high-deductible health plan). Contributions are pre-tax,
growth is tax-free, and withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are also
tax-free — a triple tax advantage no other account type offers. After age
65, non-medical withdrawals are allowed and taxed like a traditional IRA,
so an HSA can double as retirement savings for those who don't need to
spend it on healthcare along the way.

## Traditional vs. Roth: the basic tradeoff

The decision generally comes down to a comparison of tax rate now versus
expected tax rate in retirement:

- Expect a **lower** tax rate in retirement than today → traditional
  (pre-tax) contributions are usually favored, since the deduction is worth
  more now than the tax owed later.
- Expect a **similar or higher** tax rate in retirement, or want to
  diversify against future tax-law uncertainty → Roth contributions are
  usually favored.

Many financial plans intentionally hold a mix of both account types to
retain flexibility over how much taxable income to draw in any given
retirement year.

## A common priority order

A widely used (though not universal) order for directing retirement
savings, assuming high-interest debt is already handled and an emergency
fund is in place:

1. Contribute enough to a 401(k)/403(b) to capture the full employer match.
2. Max out an HSA, if eligible.
3. Pay off any remaining high-interest debt.
4. Max out an IRA (Roth or traditional, based on the tax-rate comparison
   above).
5. Return to the 401(k)/403(b) and contribute further, up to its annual
   limit.
6. Invest in a regular taxable brokerage account beyond that.

Contribution limits for 401(k)s, IRAs, and HSAs are set annually by the IRS
and change most years for inflation — always confirm the current-year
limits directly rather than relying on a prior year's figures.
