# Debt Payoff Strategies

## Two standard methods

**Avalanche method**: pay minimums on every debt, then put all extra money
toward the debt with the **highest interest rate** first. Once that's paid
off, roll its payment into the next-highest-rate debt, and so on. This
minimizes total interest paid and is mathematically optimal.

**Snowball method**: pay minimums on every debt, then put all extra money
toward the debt with the **smallest balance** first, regardless of interest
rate. Once that's paid off, roll its payment into the next-smallest balance.
This pays more in total interest than the avalanche method, but the early
wins (fully closing out an account) tend to build motivation and
follow-through for people who struggle to stay consistent with a purely
mathematical approach.

Neither is "correct" in isolation — avalanche saves more money, snowball is
often easier to stick with. The best method is the one a person will
actually follow through on.

## High-interest debt is the priority

Revolving high-interest debt (most credit cards, many personal loans) very
often carries a double-digit annual interest rate, which is higher than the
realistic long-run return most people should expect from investing. As a
rule of thumb, paying off debt at a given interest rate is equivalent to a
guaranteed, risk-free return at that same rate — a 22% APR credit card
balance is a "guaranteed 22% return" for paying it off, which safely beats
almost any investment alternative. This is why high-interest debt payoff is
usually recommended before additional investing, once a starter emergency
fund is in place.

## Low-interest debt is a different calculation

Debt with a low fixed interest rate (some mortgages, some federal student
loans, some auto loans) is a weaker candidate for aggressive extra payoff,
since the guaranteed "return" of paying it off early may be lower than a
reasonably expected long-run investment return. For this kind of debt, it's
often reasonable to pay the minimum and invest the difference instead,
though paying extra is never wrong if it reduces financial stress or if the
person simply prefers being debt-free sooner.

## A trivial balance is worth clearing outright

Small balances relative to available cash (e.g. a few hundred dollars on an
otherwise cash-flow-positive household) are usually worth paying off in
full immediately rather than "optimizing" — the interest saved from
stretching out a small payoff over many months is minor, while carrying any
revolving balance keeps accruing interest and adds an open line item to
track for no real benefit.

## Before adding new debt

Before taking on new debt, compare the interest rate against the emergency
fund and existing higher-rate balances — it rarely makes sense to hold cash
earning a low savings-account yield while carrying debt at a much higher
rate on the other side of the balance sheet.
